Resources Used

MOV (QuickTime Movie)

Originally developed by Apple for the QuickTime player, MOVs have been around since 1998, MOV is still in use today and also has support for Windows Platforms today.


Strengths:

  • Supports MIDI
  • "Fast Start" allows video to play while being downloaded.
  • Has separate tracks for audio, video, and text for further editing.
  • Allows people to combine multiple audio and video files for editing.
  • Usually high quality
  • Uses a similar compression technique to MP4.
  • Has a higher bitrate than most video files.

Issues:

  • MOV files tend to be larger than most other video formats.
  • MOV uses lossy compression, with quality being lost every time it's rendered.

MP4 (MPEG-4)

The latest version of the video file format made by the Moving Picture Experts Group, MP4 was selected as one of the main video format of HTML5.


Strengths:

  • Supports various data rates for playback on mobile devices, laptops, and desktops.
  • Allows scene manipulation and user interaction with video objects.
  • Is the standard for DVD quality video.
  • Uses lossless compression so quality isn't lost during compression.
  • Uses progressive download/pseudostreaming for easier playback on lower quality networks.

Issues:

  • Is more prone to corruption than most other video formats.
  • Google Chrome has issues handling long MP4s that use H.264 as their video codec.
  • Various browsers have issues handling MP4s with more than one audio track. Firefox only plays the last, Chrome only plays the first.

AVI (Audio Video Interweave)

A tried and true video format that's been around since 1992, AVI is still a dependable choice when it comes to video creation.


Strengths:

  • Supports MIDI
  • AVI videos have decent video and audio quality.
  • One of the best format for master files of videos copied in different formats.
  • Allows people to combine multiple audio and video files for editing.
  • Compatible with various different codecs and programs.
  • Uses lossless compression.

Issues:

  • Despite support for various codecs, the user is required to use the same codecs for playback that were used to make the AVI video.
  • Not all codecs that are compatible with AVI are high quality.
  • Not ideal for videos with subtitles.
  • Does not support user interactivity
  • AVI videos can take up to 2-3 GB for every minute of video.
  • Uses MPEG-2 compression standards, which is becoming increasingly outdated.
  • Uses lower bitrates for rendering, resulting in lower quality.

WMV (Windows Media Video)

Despite this format being exclusive to Microsoft Windows platforms, it's still commonly used and is a good choice for environments where Windows is primarily used.


Strengths:

  • Able to compress videos into smaller sizes than most other formats can.
  • Short load times for quicker playback on low-budget internet.
  • Compatible with and used by various Windows programs (The default file format for Windows Movie Maker for example).

Issues:

  • WMVs are difficult, if not impossible to access with Linux or Apple devices.

Webm Video

Despite being a newer video format, Webm is quickly catching popularity for its open-source nature and focused design for web videos exclusively.


Strengths:

  • Open-source format: Free to use and modify.
  • Supported by various web browsers, such as Firefox, Chrome, and Opera.
  • Uses only one kind of audio and video codec, guaranteeing clientside playback (unlike AVI).

Issues:

  • Doesn't always play back on mobile devices (iPhones and iPads in particular).
  • Will not work on Blackberry devices (which are mostly obsolete anyway) or Internet Explorer.
  • Only has partial support on Microsoft Edge, meaning not all of WEBM's features work for it.
